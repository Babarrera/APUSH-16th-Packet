APUSH: The South and Slavery

1790s-1850s - King Cotton and the Expansion of Slavery
Key Concept 5.2: Intensified by expansion and deepening regional divisions, debates over slavery and other economic, cultural, and political issues led the nation into civil war.

The Cotton Revolution

The invention of the cotton gin in 1793 transformed the American South and set the nation on a collision course over slavery. What was once a declining institution became the foundation of Southern wealth and identity.

The Cotton Gin's Impact

Eli Whitney's invention made cotton production massively profitable, leading to the expansion of slavery and the rise of "King Cotton" as the dominant force in the Southern economy.

Second Middle Passage

The forced migration of over 1 million enslaved people from the Upper South to the Deep South created one of the largest forced migrations in American history.

Southern Society

The Antebellum South developed a distinct social hierarchy dominated by plantation owners and built on the institution of slavery.

Major Themes

  • Economic Transformation: Cotton became the most valuable American export
  • Regional Division: North industrialized while South remained agricultural
  • Expansion of Slavery: The "peculiar institution" grew rather than died out
  • African American Community: Development of distinct culture and resistance

Historical Timeline

1793
Eli Whitney invents the cotton gin, revolutionizing cotton production and making slavery immensely profitable
1808
Congress bans international slave trade, leading to growth of domestic slave trade
1819-1820
Missouri Crisis leads to Missouri Compromise, temporarily resolving slavery expansion issue
1820s-1830s
"Second Middle Passage" - massive internal slave trade moves 1 million+ enslaved people westward
1831
Nat Turner's Rebellion - bloodiest slave revolt; leads to harsher slave codes throughout South
1830s
Abolitionist movement gains strength with Garrison's Liberator and growing northern opposition to slavery
1850
Cotton accounts for 60% of US exports; "King Cotton" doctrine fully established
