1790s-1850s: King Cotton and the Expansion of Slavery

Key Concept 5.2

Intensified by expansion and deepening regional divisions, debates over slavery and other economic, cultural, and political issues led the nation into civil war.

Chapter 10 Reading Guide

Section Supporting Details & Summary
King Cotton and Southern Expansion / Cotton and Expansion into the Old Southwest • Why "King Cotton"? Cotton became the dominant and most profitable export crop in the US, accounting for nearly 60% of American exports by the Civil War. Its economic power led Southerners like James Hammond to boast "Cotton is King," believing it gave the South immense international leverage.

• Where does the South expand? The South expanded into the "Old Southwest"—the Black Belt region of western Georgia, Alabama, Mississippi, and Louisiana. This was fueled by "Alabama Fever," a massive migration of white Southerners and their slaves after the War of 1812.
Slavery the Mainspring—Again • Is the North Innocent? No. The text states there was a "direct" connection. Northern merchants provided shipping, insurance, and brokerage for the cotton trade. Furthermore, fortunes made from the slave trade and cotton were invested in northern industrial development, such as the textile mills in Lowell, Massachusetts.
A Slave Society in a Changing World • Attitudes toward slavery hardened after the cotton gin made it incredibly profitable. While the North abolished slavery and invested in industry, railroads, and cities, the South remained a rural, agricultural society. Its capital was tied up in land and slaves, and it lagged behind in infrastructure and industrialization.
2nd Middle Passage: The Internal Slave Trade • What is this? The forced migration of over 1 million enslaved people from the Upper South (VA, MD) to the Deep South (AL, MS) to meet the labor demand of the cotton boom. This "coffle" of chained people was a brutal domestic slave trade that shattered families on a massive scale, larger than the Atlantic slave trade to North America.
The African American Community • What was life like? Despite the brutality of slavery, African Americans built a distinct and enduring culture centered on family and religion. The growth of the slave population through natural increase (not importation) and living in larger groups on plantations allowed this community to solidify.
From Cradle to Grave Slavery was a lifelong system. From a young age, children learned survival skills, including how to appear acquiescent to whites while secretly resisting. They lived with the constant fear of family separation through sale.
Field Work and the Gang System of Labor The majority of slaves (55%) worked in cotton under the "gang system," which organized them into groups working relentlessly from dawn to dusk. This was physically demanding and brutal labor.
House Servants While often better fed and clothed, house servants lived under constant white supervision and scrutiny. They had to perform "servile bows and counterfeit smiles" and had little privacy or time with their own families.
Slave Families Slave marriages, though not legally recognized, were a crucial haven of love and intimacy. Families provided a supportive kinship network, and the community acted as an extended family, using terms like "aunt" and "uncle" for other adults.
Freedom and Resistance / African American Religion Slaves resisted in both everyday and overt ways. Religion was a key outlet, blending African traditions (like call-and-response) with Christianity. Spirituals were a form of cultural expression and sometimes contained coded messages about resistance and freedom.
Slave Revolts Major revolts like Nat Turner's Rebellion (1831) in Virginia resulted in the deaths of about 60 white people. While rare, these revolts caused widespread panic in the South and led to much harsher slave codes and restrictions.
Free African Americans By 1860, there were nearly 250,000 free African Americans in the South, but their rights were severely limited by Black Codes. They could not vote, testify against whites, or carry firearms, and they lived under constant suspicion.

How inventions change history (for better and for worse) The cotton gin, invented by Eli Whitney in 1793, revolutionized the American economy but had the unintended consequence of revitalizing and expanding slavery. Before the gin, slavery was waning; after it, cotton became "king" and the demand for slave labor exploded. Before the gin, a slave could clean only 1 pound of cotton a day. After the gin, a slave could clean 300 to 1,000 pounds a day. The slave population grew from 700,000 in 1790 to 4 million in 1860, directly fueled by the profitability of cotton.
Second Middle Passage The "Second Middle Passage" was the massive, forced internal slave trade within the United States that moved over a million enslaved people from the Upper South to the Deep South. This trade, which was larger than the transatlantic slave trade to the U.S., was driven by the cotton boom and shattered countless black families. Enslaved people were often chained together in groups called "coffles" and forced to walk hundreds of miles to new plantations. Families were routinely separated at auctions, with individuals sold to the highest bidder with little regard for keeping parents and children together.

3 Must Knows about the Era of Good Feelings A period of heightened nationalism and political unity (only one party: Democratic-Republicans) during President James Monroe's terms (1817-1825). Marked by Henry Clay's American System (internal improvements, national bank, tariff) and the Monroe Doctrine. The name is misleading; beneath the surface, sectionalism was growing over issues like slavery (Missouri Compromise) and the tariff, and the Panic of 1819 caused economic turmoil.
3 Must Knows about the Monroe Doctrine A foreign policy statement (1823) warning European powers not to interfere in the Western Hemisphere or colonize further. It was issued due to fears of Spanish reconquest of new Latin American republics and Russian expansion on the Pacific coast. It became a cornerstone of US foreign policy, asserting US dominance in the Americas and was later used by presidents like Teddy Roosevelt to justify intervention.
3 Must Knows about the Abolition Movement Grew from the religious and moral fervor of the Second Great Awakening, which emphasized perfecting society and framed slavery as a sin. Key abolitionists included William Lloyd Garrison (immediate emancipation), Frederick Douglass (powerful ex-slave orator), and the Grimké Sisters (Southerners who spoke out). Abolitionists used various tactics, including newspapers (The Liberator, North Star), public lectures, and appeals to religion and the Declaration of Independence to argue their cause.
What Was the Missouri Compromise? It was a temporary political solution to the crisis of Missouri applying for statehood as a slave state, which would have upset the balance of power between free and slave states in the Senate. The compromise preserved the balance by admitting Missouri as a slave state and Maine as a free state, and it banned slavery in the rest of the Louisiana Territory north of the 36°30' parallel. It postponed the Civil War but did not resolve the underlying issue of slavery. It highlighted the deep sectional divisions that would only intensify in the coming decades.
